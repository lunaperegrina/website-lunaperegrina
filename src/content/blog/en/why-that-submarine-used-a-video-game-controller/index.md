---

When the news came out that a submarine was being controlled with a **video game controller**, many people had the same reaction:

> "This can only be an absurdly bad idea."

The controller in question was a **Logitech F710**, a wireless gamepad released around 2010.

At first glance, it looks like a hacky workaround.
But surprisingly, **this choice is not as absurd as it seems**.

## The controller used

The device shown in the photos was the **Logitech F710**, a relatively old controller.

Even so, it has some important characteristics:

- familiar layout (console style)
- analog sticks
- multiple configurable buttons
- wireless communication
- low cost
- easy replacement

In other words, it is a **simple, reliable, and widely known input interface**.

## Video game controllers are used in real systems

Using game controllers to operate complex machines **is not exclusive to this submarine**.

This kind of interface has appeared in several real-world contexts.

For example:

- the **U.S. military** has used **Xbox 360 controllers** to operate submarine periscope systems
- drones and robots often use **gamepad-based interfaces**
- experimental weapon systems and remote platforms have used **console controllers**

The main reason is simple.

## Interface familiarity

Gamepads have a big advantage: **millions of people already know how to use them**.

That means:

- smaller learning curve
- intuitive operation
- precise analog control
- less need for specialized training

For tasks such as moving cameras, controlling orientation, or handling remote vehicles, the dual-stick model works very well.

Basically, it is a **human interface refined over decades of video games**.

## Another factor: cost and simplicity

Another reason to use commercial controllers is cost.

Building proprietary hardware involves:

- design
- certification
- manufacturing
- maintenance

Meanwhile, a video game controller:

- is cheap
- is easy to replace
- already has drivers and support across many systems

In many cases, it is used only as an **input device**, while the software behind it does all the actual control logic.

## So was the idea good?

The interface itself, using a gamepad, **is not necessarily absurd**.

In critical systems, the real problems are usually elsewhere:

- structural engineering
- system redundancy
- operational safety
- hardware validation
- certification

So yes, the controller may look strange, but **it is not necessarily the critical engineering issue**.

Using a video game controller to operate complex machines sounds like a joke at first.

But from a **human-machine interface** perspective, it makes a lot of sense.

Gamepads are:

- familiar
- precise
- affordable
- widely available

That is why they end up showing up in unexpected places, from near the Titanic to your living room TV (if someone is playing Wii Sports bowling without the safety strap).
